  • Visas:<\/b> There are several categories and subcategories of visas. <\/span>Immigrant visas <\/b>are for people who intend to live permanently in the U.S. while <\/span>non-immigrant visas<\/b> are for people who are staying in the U.S. temporarily. Non-immigrant visas include types for tourism, work, education, and others.<\/span><\/li>\n
  • Green cards:<\/b> A green card is an important step in the journey toward full citizenship. When you are issued a green card, you are granted full legal status, meaning that you can legally live and work in the U.S. There are several ways to obtain a green card \u2013 for example, being sponsored by a resident family member or employer, or applying for legal asylum.<\/span><\/li>\n

  • Adjustment of status:<\/b> If you meet certain criteria and you possess a temporary visa, you can apply for an adjustment of status, which can allow you to obtain a green card.<\/span><\/li>\n
  • DACA:<\/b> Signed into law in 2012, Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) provides some level of protection for undocumented children who grew up in the U.S. If you meet certain residency criteria, you can apply for DACA relief, which will protect you from deportation and allow you to obtain a driver\u2019s license and other forms of documentation.<\/span><\/li>\n

    Immigration Counsel for Families in North Carolina<\/h3>

    The area of family-based immigration encompasses a wide range of cases in which United States citizens and lawful permanent residents (\"green card<\/a>\" holders) seek to bring their family members and future spouses to the U.S. legally. According to U.S. immigration law, lawful permanent residents and legal citizens can serve as sponsors to help their relatives obtain permanent or temporary visas<\/a>. <\/p>

    Generally, the application process for family-based immigration is relatively straightforward. The process begins when a citizen or permanent resident files a completed Petition for Alien Relative (Form I-130) with the primary immigration agency of the U.S. government: United States Citizenship and Immigration Services, also known as USCIS. Once the petition is accepted by USCIS, the petitioner will be notified of a date after which the family members in question are able to apply for visas or green cards. <\/p>

    Johnson County Lawyers Who Understand the Preference System for Family-Based Immigration<\/h3>

    The United States government allows for the approval of a limited number of family visas and permanent resident status applications in a given year. Therefore, a preference system has been created for processing family immigration requests. Legal spouses, minor children, and parents of current U.S. citizens over the age of 21 are immediate relatives<\/a>, which means they are not included in the family preference system. <\/p>

    Applications for other relatives, however, are considered in order based on the status of the petitioner and the relationship:<\/p>

    • First preference is given to petitions filed by U.S. citizens for their unmarried children over the age of 21.<\/li>
    • Second preference is given to petitions filed by permanent residents for their spouses and children. Minor children receive preference over adult children. <\/li>
    • Third preference is given to petitions filed by U.S. citizens for their married children and the immediate relatives of their married children. <\/li>
    • Fourth preference is given to petitions filed by U.S. citizens for their siblings and the immediate relatives of their siblings. <\/li><\/ul>

      A family-based immigration petition will not be considered for grandparents, aunts, uncles, cousins, or in-laws.<\/p>
